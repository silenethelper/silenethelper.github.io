What are ferromagnetic domains?

Ans: A ferromagnetic substance consists of many microscopic regions called magnetic domains. Each domain consists of atomic magnetic moments that aligned parallel to each other, constituting a resultant magnetic moment. However, the magnetic moments of different domains orient randomly so that resultant magnetic moment of the material is zero. Hence, ferromagnetic material is observed non magnetic in absence of applied magnetic filed. When an external field is applied, magnetic moments of domains tend to align along the applied field, resulting strong magnetic field. Saturation of magnetization is due to the complete alignment of moments of all domains in same direction. When magnetizing field is removed, there is a tendency of domain to stay they have been aligned. This results from remanance field and hysteresis effect.

What is retentivity and coarcivity of a ferromagnetic material?

Ans: Retentivity of a ferromagnetic material is a measure of magnetism remaining in a specimen when the magnetizing field is reduced to zero. Greater the retentivity, the stronger is the magnetization of the material. The coarcivity is the reverse magnetic field needed to reduce the magnetism to zero in the material. Greater the coercive force, the better able the rod to keep its magnetization despite the presence of strong magnetic fields.

A paramagnetic above Curie temperature. Why?

Ans: A ferromagnetic material follows Curie law and the alignment of atomic magnetic moments in it is disturbed with increase of temperature. Above the Curie temperature, this alignment is completely random due to thermal motion of the atoms and the material becomes paramagnetic.
