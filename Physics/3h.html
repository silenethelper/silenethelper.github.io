How will you verify that light coming from the sky is partially polarised?

Ans. We can verify this by placing a piece of polaroid sunglass in front of our eye and rotate it as we look at the sky on a clear day. We will notice a change in light intensity with the orientation of the polaroid. For a certain orientation, a dark band will appear in the sky, showing that light from the region is strongly polarised.

How can one distinguish between an unpolarised light beam and a linearly polarised beam using a polaroid?

Ans: When a polaroid is rotated in the path of unpolarised light, the intensity of the light transmitted from the polaroid remains undiminished. However, when the polaroid is rotated in the path of plane polarised light, its intensity will vary from maximum (when the vibrations of the plane polarised light are parallel to the axis of the polaroid) to minimum (when the direction of vibrations becomes perpendicular to the axis of the crystal).

What is Brewster's law?

Ans: Brewster's law states that the tangent of polarizing angle is equal to the refractive index of the material i.e. u = tan Op Since, the refractive index of the material u varies with wavelength of light, so polarizing angle Op is also the function of wavelength.
